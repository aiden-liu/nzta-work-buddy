## Document Purpose (Agreed)

Section 12 exists to establish a **consistent monitoring and alerting baseline** that makes the Data Platform governable, auditable, and safe to operate at scale.

The root cause it addresses is **inconsistency** — which cascades into governance gaps, which enables silent failures.

### Primary Failure Modes (explicitly addressed)
1. **Silent failures** — pipelines fail or produce bad data with no detection
2. **Drift without detection** — pipelines technically succeed but data meaning quietly degrades (sum mismatches, late arrivals, quality drift). No alert fires because nothing "broke". Named separately from silent failures because it requires different detection strategies.
3. **Governance gaps** — no audit trail to prove monitoring was in place
4. **Inconsistency** — every product team monitors differently, making platform-wide visibility impossible (root cause of the above)

### Intended Readers & Core Needs

| Role | Core Need |
|---|---|
| **Project Developer** | What must I instrument before my code ships, and how do I make late data / quality drift / sum mismatches detectable without building it from scratch each time? |
| **Platform Engineer** | What's the minimum viable monitoring baseline that gives platform-wide visibility without requiring me to understand every product team's domain? |
| **Governance Reviewer** | Can I prove — not just assume — that this pipeline's data is compliant? (downstream consumption visibility / Purview → deferred to separate governance section) |
| **Incident Responder** | Is the alert telling me what broke, where, and what the blast radius is — or am I starting from zero? |

### Scope Decisions
- **MS Purview** (lineage, downstream consumption visibility) → deferred to a separate governance section
- **"Drift without detection"** → named as a distinct failure mode in the document purpose statement

**Key decisions:**
- Review cadence: annually at minimum + triggered by significant platform or organisational changes
- Post-mortems: when they occur, monitoring assessment is included — standard doesn't mandate post-mortem culture (that's incident management, not monitoring)
- Governance reporting: dropped entirely — no governance forums exist at NZTA, won't reference structures that don't exist
- Dashboard review: dropped — redundant with 12.3 low severity review cadence, different usage would create confusion
- Dropped editorial line about "compliance theatre" — too opinionated for a standards document

**Agreed draft text:**

> Monitoring and alerting standards are reviewed annually at minimum, and triggered by significant platform or organisational changes — such as new tooling adoption, team restructuring, or major pipeline redesigns.
>
> When post-mortems occur following incidents, they include an assessment of whether monitoring and alerting coverage was adequate — specifically whether the right alerts existed, fired in time, carried sufficient context, and reached the right people. Lessons are fed back into the standard or the implementation guide as appropriate.

### 12.3 Alerting & Escalation

**Key decisions:**
- ServiceNow as incident management and routing mechanism — handles ownership assignment implicitly
- Critical response → business hours only (no on-call rotation at NZTA currently)
- Four severity levels defined by impact/urgency characteristics, not numeric SLA targets (out of scope per ticket)
- Low severity alerts reviewed regularly — alerts producing no action are tuned or suppressed
- Runbook ownership follows two-tier model: platform team for infrastructure/platform service alerts; domain team for pipeline/data product alerts
- Declarative tone consistent with 12.4
- "An alert that fires constantly without producing action is a misconfigured alert, not a monitoring success" — explicit challenge to alert fatigue

**Agreed draft text:**

> Alerting translates monitoring signals into timely, actionable notifications that reach the right people with enough context to respond effectively. Alerts integrate with **ServiceNow** for incident management, ownership assignment, and escalation tracking.
>
> **Alert severity levels** are defined by impact and urgency:
>
> | Level | Characteristics | Response |
> |---|---|---|
> | **Critical** | Platform or pipeline completely down, data unavailable, production impacted, or SLO breach occurring | Immediate response during business hours; ServiceNow incident auto-created |
> | **High** | Significant degradation, data delayed or partially incorrect, risk of downstream impact if unresolved | Prompt response; ServiceNow incident created; domain team notified |
> | **Medium** | Non-critical anomaly, pipeline degraded but still producing, investigation warranted | Reviewed within business hours; ServiceNow ticket created |
> | **Low** | Informational, no immediate impact, trend worth monitoring | Logged and reviewed on a regular cadence; alerts that consistently produce no action are tuned or suppressed |
>
> **Runbooks** exist for all critical alerts, defining investigation and remediation steps. Runbook ownership follows the two-tier model — the platform team maintains runbooks for infrastructure and platform service alerts; domain teams maintain runbooks for pipeline and data product alerts.
>
> **Alert noise** is actively managed. Thresholds are tuned and correlated to prevent alert fatigue — an alert that fires constantly without producing action is a misconfigured alert, not a monitoring success. Low severity alerts are reviewed regularly and either escalated, tuned, or suppressed.

### 12.4 Data Pipeline Health

**Key decisions:**
- Three detection categories: execution health (must, baseline today), volume health (should, domain team defines thresholds), correctness health (should, domain team defines rules)
- Declarative tone throughout — no "must/should" modal verbs except where platform obligation is clear
- Alerts are primary detection mechanism, not dashboards
- Dashboards serve team leads and platform oversight — investigation tool, not frontline monitoring
- Alert context fields mandated: pipeline ID, failure stage, expected vs actual values, last successful run, correlation ID
- Platform team provides reusable frameworks; domain teams engage to define rules — shared contract
- "Monitoring without domain-defined rules is incomplete" — balancing statement to avoid platform-only bias
- Detection gap acknowledged: currently reliant on end consumers; this section directly addresses that
- DQX and Open Data Contract Standards — noted as in-flight platform work, not referenced in standard

**Agreed draft text:**

> Pipeline health monitoring covers three categories, in order of increasing difficulty and domain specificity:
>
> - **Execution health** — whether the pipeline ran, completed within expected duration, and exited without errors. This is the minimum baseline, monitored for all pipelines across all environments.
> - **Volume health** — whether the expected amount of data arrived. Automated alerts trigger on unexpected record count drops, missing source files, processing backlogs, or late data arrival beyond defined thresholds. Volume thresholds should be defined and registered in Unity Catalog by the domain team.
> - **Correctness health** — whether the data is semantically valid. Quality validation results (see Section 8) are integrated into pipeline health monitoring. Correctness rules are defined by the domain team and enforced within the pipeline; failures surface as alerts rather than being silently swallowed.
>
> Automated alerts are the primary detection mechanism for pipeline health. Alerts carry sufficient context for an engineer to begin investigation immediately — including pipeline identifier, failure stage, expected vs actual values, last successful run time, and a correlation identifier.
>
> Observability dashboards provide pipeline health visibility for team leads and platform oversight — serving as an investigation and situational awareness tool rather than a substitute for automated alerting.
>
> The platform team provides reusable frameworks for volume and correctness health monitoring to ensure detection capability is consistent across pipelines and independent of individual project delivery timelines. Domain teams engage with these frameworks to define and maintain rules for their data products. Monitoring without domain-defined rules is incomplete.

### 12.2 Metrics & Telemetry

**Definitions (agreed):**
- **Metrics** — quantified, time-series measurements derived from aggregating raw events, used to understand system behaviour over time and trigger automated responses when thresholds are breached
- **Telemetry** — the continuous, automated collection and transmission of observability data (metrics, logs, traces) from systems to a centralised platform. It is the *pipeline* that carries the signal — alerting is one consumer of that signal, not the same thing
- Alerting qualities (timely, accurate, only notify when necessary) → belong in 12.3, not here
- Telemetry qualities → complete, consistent, low-latency, tamper-evident

**Why a standard is needed:**
- Without it: teams emit different metrics with different names → no platform-wide aggregation
- Inconsistent retention → audit fails when needed most (post-incident)
- No shared baseline of "normal" → alert thresholds set in isolation

**Structure decision:**
- Standard (Section 12) → what must be true, abstract level
- Implementation Guide (Section-12-Implementation-Guide.md) → tool-specific detail per ADF, Databricks, ADLS, Power BI
- No inline italic examples in the standard — use a single cross-reference line to the guide instead

**Additional decisions (agreed):**
- Tracing → logs currently siloed, no end-to-end correlation exists today. Standard requires correlation ID (run_id, pipeline_id) as minimum; full distributed tracing is a platform maturity goal
- Freshness/completeness thresholds → "should be defined and registered in Unity Catalog, typically by the domain team" — soft framing intentional given weak data product ownership today
- 180-day retention → cross-reference Section 4.4 only, no restatement
- Telemetry scope → all three environments (Dev, Test, Prod); retention applies to Prod only
- Standard vs implementation guide → single cross-reference line at end of 12.2, no inline examples

**Agreed draft text:**

> Metrics are quantified, time-series measurements derived from aggregating raw events, used to understand system behaviour over time and trigger automated responses when thresholds are breached. Telemetry is the continuous, automated collection and transmission of observability data from systems to a centralised platform — it is the pipeline that carries the signal. Alerting, dashboards, and audit trails are all consumers of that signal.
>
> Telemetry must be active across all environments (Dev, Test, Prod). Retention requirements apply to Production only — telemetry data must be retained for a minimum of 180 days to support audit and forensic analysis (see Section 4.4).
>
> Metrics & telemetry must cover the three pillars of observability (see Section 4):
> - Metrics — system utilisation, job success/failure, error rates, latency, throughput, and queue depths
> - Logs — structured logs emitted by all pipelines and services in alignment with Section 4.1
> - Traces — all pipelines must emit a consistent correlation identifier (e.g. run_id, pipeline_id) to enable future end-to-end trace correlation across platform services. Full distributed tracing is a platform maturity goal.
>
> Data freshness and completeness metrics must be collected to validate the timeliness and completeness of ingestion and processing. Freshness and completeness thresholds should be defined and registered in Unity Catalog, typically by the domain team responsible for the data product.
>
> Telemetry must be reliable, consistent, and tamper-evident. If telemetry is absent or broken, all downstream consumers — alerts, dashboards, audit trails — are blind. Telemetry health is therefore a first-class platform concern.
>
> See the [Section 12 Implementation Guide] for tool-specific metric examples across ADF, Databricks, ADLS, and Power BI.

### 12.1 Monitoring Scope

**Ownership model (two-tier):**
- Platform team → accountable for infrastructure + platform service monitoring AND for providing shared monitoring frameworks/tooling that domain/project teams use
- Domain team → accountable for pipeline and data product monitoring WITHIN the frameworks the platform team provides
- The seam between layers (e.g. ADF triggering Databricks) → platform team responsibility
- Monitoring ownership must be formally transferred from project team to domain team upon project completion

**Environment monitoring requirements:**

| Environment | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Dev | Logging must be present; alerting optional |
| Test | Logging required; alerting on pipeline failures required |
| Prod | Full monitoring; all critical alerts active and routed to ServiceNow |

**Dropped:** "higher rigour in UAT and Production" — too vague, replaced with explicit per-environment requirements above
